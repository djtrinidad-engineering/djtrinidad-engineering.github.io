Mode Shapes Manual Computation for a 3-Storey Concrete Structure

Published: January 2026 | Read Time: 5 min

They say in the age of software, manual calculations are obsolete. I disagree.

This week, I revisited the fundamentals of structural dynamics by manually computing the mode shapes of a 3-storey concrete structure—pen, paper (digital in this case), and the classic eigenvalue problem. Then, I verified these hand-calculated mode shapes against ETABS results.

📐 Mode 1 (First Translational Mode): Shape resembles a straight line or gentle curve bending upward.
📐 Mode 2 (Second Translational Mode): Building bends into an S-shape or curved shape with reversal.
📐 Mode 3 (Torsional/Rotational Mode): Creates "snaking" or "wavy" deformation pattern.

Why does this matter? Because in a high-seismic region like the Philippines—where we've felt multiple significant earthquakes just this last quarter of 2025—understanding how and why structures vibrate is not academic. It's essential.

As structural engineers, we must:
✅ Grasp the physics behind the software outputs
✅ Validate computer models with first-principles checks
✅ Ensure our designs respond correctly under dynamic loads

The basics of earthquake engineering and structural dynamics form our first line of defense against seismic events.

Mode Shapes Manual Computation for a 3-Storey Concrete Structure

Published: January 2026 | Read Time: 5 min

This week in my Bridge Engineering lecture, we dove into the core of determinate analysis: Influence Lines.

We manually calculated the maximum positive moment and shear for a propped beam under AASHTO HL-93 live loading at a specific section. Using carefully drawn influence lines, we positioned the truck loads for the worst-case effect. Then, we ran the same model through MIDAS Civil NX for a moving load analysis.

Hand calculations vs. sophisticated software showed discrepancies of only 2-3 kN—a testament to the reliability of engineering fundamentals of concepts like influence lines when applied meticulously to validate the output.

This exercise wasn’t just about getting the right answer. It was about:
✅ Reinforcing the “why” behind the code provisions.
✅ Building intuition for load paths and structural response.
✅ Reminding future engineers that software is a tool, not a substitute for fundamental understanding.

As a full-time structural engineer in a private firm and a part-time faculty member teaching bridge engineering, I believe this blend of theory and practice is essential. We shape not just structures, but also the minds that will build the future.

Proud to be bridging the gap between academia and industry, one calculation at a time.

Buckling Analysis

Published: August 2025 | Read Time: 5 min

When designing a structure, strength and stiffness are the common characteristics engineers use to determine whether the structural members are safe. However, it is also important to check for stability.

Slender members may have high strength capacities and stiffness, but they might be considered unstable due to buckling under significant compression loads. Buckling is a common occurrence for compressive members such as slender columns.

The Euler buckling formula is used to determine the critical load that will cause buckling of the column.

To demonstrate buckling analysis, software with finite element analysis capabilities such as midasGen can be utilized. (See images attached in this post). The objective of the analysis is to determine the buckling mode shapes and its corresponding critical compressive load. As can be seen in the images below, the output of the analysis is the critical load factor that will be multiplied to the compressive force and determine the permissible load for buckling stress.

For columns, it buckles into different mode shapes. The usual mode shapes are single-curvature and double-curvature. These are illustrated as Mode 1, and Mode 2, respectively. As for Mode 3, it can be observed that three (3) curvatures are present. Correlating this with the critical load factor output for different modes, we can state that as the number of modes increases, the critical load factor also increases -- which means a higher compressive load is needed for the column to undergo such mode failure.

In this case, since the example does not have intermediate restraints, it is safe enough to say that the critical force we should consider in designing the column is from Mode 1.
